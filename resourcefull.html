Overview

Challenge

My team of three designers was challenged to create a digital product to help the library strengthen its community. The assignment was vague which opened a lot of doors for the research. One thing we knew for sure is that most public libraries are already adapting to digital standards and should not stop.

“Today we are in the midst of a tremendous shift in the way Americans consume literature and other content, but one thing has not changed — the library must continue to play a central role in providing open and free access to information and ideas.”
- Huffington Post

Outcome

In the process of our research, we discovered a unique cognitive dissonance between what users believed and how they acted that led us to the creation of a plugin that can connect the user and the library.

Research

Competition

The first obstacle was to find who presents competition to the library. We decided to push off the library mission.

“We welcome and support all people in their enjoyment of reading and pursuit of lifelong learning. Working together, we strive to provide equal access to information, ideas, and knowledge through books, programs, and other resources. We believe in the freedom to read, to learn, to discover.”
- Chicago Public Library

So, library purpose seems to rotate around providing access to information, ideas, and knowledge. Although the main competitor of the library is any search engine, we focused on the companies that provide access to information and books, such as Amazon, Google, and Barnes&Nobles.

We did a SWOT analysis and here are the areas where the competitors were coming shortly which would be a set of opportunities for the library:

Amazon - paid access
Google - too much information, lack of credibility
Barnes & Noble - paid access, limited to books

What we saw is that there are so much information and so many resources offered that there is no way to tell what is real and what is not without additional searches.

SMEs interviews

Given how abstract our challenge was we needed to talk to some experts to further narrow down the problems. As a team, we have spoken with a total of six librarians fulfilling different roles in different libraries. We have chosen libraries of different scale and locations to give us a better general picture. The problems that stood out and looked promising:

Key findings:

  • Shortage of teachers/volunteers
  • Small budget
  • Poor website design
  • Trouble reaching young professionals
“Young professionals are the hardest group to reach. Once they are out of college and until they have kids, there is not much that interests them in the library”.
- Gina, Digital Resources Manager at Boulder Public Library.

User research

Why is it hard to reach young professionals?

We were onto something. We needed to understand what brings regular patrons (all ages) to the library, what do young professionals believe library offers and what challenges they encounter that library could potentially offer solutions for.

We conducted a survey with 108 people filtering them by the frequency of the library usage and found the following:

  • 98% of users use the internet multiple times a day
  • 66% of users use the library a few times a year or never
  • 52% of users say the internet replaces the library

One of the survey subjects gave us a lead. This is how he explained the main reason why he didn’t use the library much:

“There’s just too much to keep up with right now. It’s busy. Yeah”.
- Phillip

Interviewing 6 young professionals helped us to further narrow down problem areas.

Key problems

  • Lack of knowledge of library services
  • Prefer to interact online
  • Value convenience
  • Inconvenient hours
“I think one of the things that keep me using the library is that you can really trust the information that is there. Or I can compare it more objectively to the information I’m getting from other sources”.
- Michael, 33 years old

Problem

Additional research

As a team, we decided to dig deeper into the whole credibility issue. Additional research helped us to define the following dissonance:

  • 87% of millennials think that libraries help find information that is trustworthy and reliable. - Belief
  • 60% of Pew Research Center study participants, agreed that it is difficult to know whether information found online is trustworthy. - Need
  • 66% of millennials interviewed by us prefer using the Internet and don’t use library services because they find them inconvenient.
    - Contradicting behavior

Why are library trust and usage is on a rise for millennials, and yet millennials we were talking too didn't use it?

Problem statement

The internet is a great resource for providing information quickly, but it’s not always credible. Young professionals need a flexible, reliable and fast way to learn more about their interests from sources they trust.

Ideating

Extension

At first, we thought that it should be mobile since most young professionals seem to spend the most time on their phone. But a person wants to have only that many apps, why compete with FB and google for the space on the user's phone. At least not until the product is established.
Website Wasn’t hitting a spot either. Library already has a website and since we were challenged with digital product design and not service design we had to let go.
And that’s when we thought of extension. This would allow us to meet user right where they need us. They read something online, they doubt it and they can get help from the library right there.

Prototyping and usability testing


Link to the full prototype

Users interested in a topic can search via the extensions to find credible resources on said topic.

If a user’s search returns no results, they can send a request for credible information.

If a user thinks a source is non-credible, they have the option of reporting it for review.

Test results

  • Users have completed all the tasks
  • Most participants felt that the information provided was relatively credible, there was slight hesitation trusting it and a few were less trusting.
  • Request feature takes too long which wouldn’t always encourage the user to employ it.

Moving forward

Recommendations:

  • Focus on how information is curated
  • Adding validation method such as reCaptcha
  • Add or transform the current request feature into a live chat with a librarian
